In This Text Set

This text set includes four informational texts and an interactive map. All focus on an aspect of culture and the way culture shapes our lives.

What Is Culture? is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign What Is Culture? for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text structures and features appear across the texts in this set.

What Is Culture?

by Elena Knowles
Informational Text - Word Count: 547   Lexile: 760L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective Students will understand what culture is and how culture affects people’s lives.

  • Language Objective Students will describe several aspects of culture, using examples.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a description text structure lists features and examples to explain an idea.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs illustrate complex ideas.

Summary

This text explains key aspects of culture, using a “cultural iceberg” model to explain how some parts of culture may be invisible to a casual observer.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What is culture? Ask: What would you like to learn about culture? What ideas do you have about what culture is?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words culture, beliefs, and heritage. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Have you heard people talk about their culture? What were they talking about? Does your family have traditions, such as holidays you celebrate or special foods you eat? Can you give an example of a belief that you or someone you know has?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in What Is Culture? might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When you influence someone, you have an effect on them. Who are some people who influence your decisions?

Vocabulary Four-square

Model creating a vocabulary four-square with students. Explain the four elements of the square: a definition of a word, an example of the word in a sentence, a synonym or antonym for the word, and an example. Say: How would you define culture? Could you use culture in a sentence? What is a synonym for culture? What is an example of a culture? Tell students to create a four-square for culture on a sheet of paper. A completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that this text has a descriptive text structure. Say: This kind of text describes or defines something by giving examples and details. Have students read screen 3 (Printable Text p. 2). Ask: How does the author define culture? (the way we live our lives) What are some examples of culture? (Possible responses: food, language, holidays, music, dance)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “gives examples,” “explains something”

Text Features Say: This text uses photographs to illustrate different aspects of culture. Have students preview the photographs and use them to identify examples of culture. Then, point out the iceberg photograph on screen 5 (Printable Text p. 4). Say: The author compares different aspects of culture to the parts of an iceberg. How do you think culture is like an iceberg? How does the photograph help you understand this comparison?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “show examples,” “make a comparison”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read What Is Culture?, have them review their completed four-squares. Ask: Are there any squares you had a hard time filling in? If time permits, invite classmates to share their four-squares and add to them based on their classmates’ responses.

Display these images from What Is Culture? to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as culture, tradition, and identify.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between What Is Culture? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What have you read about traditional foods, dance, or music? How did the [foods, dance, music] relate to a particular culture?
  • What Is Culture? describes how culture affects people’s beliefs or values. What else have you read about this topic? What are some examples of how culture affects people’s values?
  • The writer describes how cultures can have different expectations or unspoken rules. Have you read any books about people who need to adjust to a different culture? What happens in these books?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, display the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have students fill in the What Is Culture? row with something they learned about aspects of culture.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are some examples of culture in your life?
  • What parts of culture are easy to identify? What parts are harder to see?
  • What are some examples of cultures in the United States?

Write About The Big Question

Remind students of the description of the cultural iceberg in the text. Ask them to draw an iceberg diagram for culture in their lives. Remind them that the top part of the iceberg includes parts of their culture that are easy to see, such as holidays they celebrate and languages they speak. The other parts, such as how they talk to others, aren’t as easy to see.

What Is My Culture?

by Zach Petersen
Informational Text - Word Count: 655   Lexile: 720L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize the different cultures that play a role in their lives.

  • Language Objective Students will identify their culture and how it affects their lives.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a description text structure explains features and examples in order to describe or define something.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section headings help readers identify the main topics or ideas in a text.

Summary

This text explains the variety of cultures that may be part of kids’ lives, including national, local, and family cultures.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What is culture? Ask: How do you think What Is My Culture? will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words regional, beliefs, and heritage. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What region, or part of the country, do you live in? How would you describe the regional culture? A belief is something you believe in. What is an example of a belief? If you wanted to know about your family’s heritage or history, who would you ask?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in What Is My Culture? might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: An aspect is an element or part of something. What is an aspect of your culture? How do your friends or family influence you, or have an effect on you?

Vocabulary Four-square

Model creating a vocabulary four-square graphic organizer with students. Explain the elements of the square: a word in the center, with a definition of the word, an example of the word in a sentence, synonyms for the word, and an example in four squares around the center. Have students complete a four-square diagram for the word heritage, based on the text. A completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that What Is My Culture? uses a descriptive text structure. Say: A descriptive text explains something by listing characteristics and giving examples. This text explains characteristics and examples of people’s cultures. Tell students that descriptive texts often use clue words such as for example and such as.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “gives examples,” “explains something”

Text Features Remind students that headings are bold words that introduce a section of the text. Say: Writers use headings to organize their ideas. Often, the headings help show what each section will be about. Have students preview the heading on screen 3 (Printable Text p. 2). Ask: What would you expect to read about in this section?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “organize ideas,” “introduce topic of the section”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read What Is My Culture?, have them review their completed four-squares and share them with a partner. Ask: Are there any details you would like to add?

Display these images from What Is My Culture? to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as regional, culture, and heritage.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between What Is My Culture? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • Have you read other texts about people learning about their culture? What did they learn?
  • What Is My Culture? describes cultures in different areas, such as regions and towns. What have you read about the culture where you live?
  • Think about the cultural iceberg from What Is Culture? Which parts of your culture are at the top part of the cultural iceberg, which is easy to see? Which parts are hard to see, but still important?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the What Is My Culture? row with something they learned about their culture.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What is your family’s culture or heritage? How is your family’s culture a part of your life?
  • What are some cultures in our region of the United States? Are you a part of these cultures? Why or why not?
  • Do people have more than one culture? Why or why not?

Write About The Big Question

Have students write a response to 2-3 of the questions at the end of the text. Their responses should describe an aspect of culture in their lives, using examples.

Cultures in the United States

by Ella Riley
Map - Word Count: 593   Lexile: 940L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize cultural influences of different groups on regions of the United States.

  • Language Objective Students will describe elements of culture in different regions of the United States.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast text structure explains similarities and differences between places.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will use information from a map to explain ideas about culture in the United States.

Summary

This map identifies unique cultures from all around the United States and describes the influence of diverse groups on each region.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: What is culture? Ask: How do you think Cultures in the United States will help you think about the Big Question? What are some cultures in the United States that you know about?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words region, custom, and heritage. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What region of the country do we live in? Can you think of a custom or tradition that you might see if you visited another country? Your heritage is your cultural history, or roots. What is your family’s heritage?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Cultures in the United States might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: How do immigrants contribute or give back to our community? What are some different groups that influence or affect life in our region?

Vocabulary Four-square

Review how to fill in a vocabulary four-square with students. Explain that the vocabulary word is in the center. The other four elements of the square, around the vocabulary word, include a definition of the word, a sentence that uses the word, a synonym or antonym for the word, and an example. Tell students to complete a four-square diagram for region as they read the text. Their completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that the map includes descriptions of the cultures in different places, so students can compare and contrast. Read the descriptions for Minnesota and San Francisco. Then ask: How are these places similar? (Possible response: In both places, immigrants have influenced the local culture.) How are they different? (Possible response: Immigrants came to the regions from different areas.)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare-and-Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare-and-contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains how places are similar and different”

Text Features Say: This writer chose to give the information on a map. What are some of the features of this map? Guide students to identify the labels for cities and states. Then, have students find the location closest to your community and read the information. Ask: How does the map help you understand the information differently than just reading it in a text?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Maps.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how maps help readers understand the text. For example: “show locations,” “help readers understand distance between places”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Cultures in the United States, have them review and share their completed four-squares for the word region. Ask them to add details based on their classmates’ responses.

Display these images from Cultures in the United States to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as custom, region, and heritage.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Cultures in the United States and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What else have you read about the places shown on the map? What new things did you learn from Cultures in the United States?
  • In Cultures in the United States, you read about cultures in different U.S. regions. What else have you read or heard about regional cultures?
  • What are some examples of culture you read about in What Is Culture? Which of these examples were described in Cultures in the United States?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Cultures in the United States row with something they learned about regional cultures.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What did you learn about cultures in the United States? What were some facts that surprised you?
  • What other cultures in the United States can you think of? What cultures in the United States have you seen in your community?

Write About the Big Question

Have students write an entry for the map describing the culture of their community or a community they have visited. Remind students of aspects of the regional or local culture they can include, such as music, history, dance, food, or architecture.

How Do We Talk About Culture?

by Margot Boyd
Informational Text - Word Count: 657   Lexile: 840

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective Students will understand and compare ideas about how immigrants have contributed to culture in the United States.

  • Language Objective Students will explain and use metaphors to describe culture in the United States.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast text structure describes similarities and differences between ideas.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs illustrate or further explain key concepts in a text.

Summary

This text explores how immigrants contribute to culture in the United States, using images such as a melting pot and a patchwork quilt.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What is culture? Say: This text is about different ways people talk about culture. Why is it important to talk about culture? How does talking about culture help us understand what it is?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words immigrants, ancestors, and tradition. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: An ancestor is a relative or family member from long ago. What do you know about your ancestors, or the ancestors of people you know? Were they immigrants who came to the United States or another country? What is one of your family’s traditions or customs?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in How Do We Talk About Culture? might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: How do people adapt or adjust to life in a new place? Why do people compare the United States to a melting pot? How have immigrants influenced, or changed, their communities?

VOCABULARY FOUR-SQUARE

Review with students how to make a vocabulary four-square, with a vocabulary word in the center. Explain the four other elements of the square: a definition of the word, an example of the word in a sentence, a synonym or antonym, and an example. Tell students to complete a four-square diagram for ancestors, based on the text. Invite them to use reference materials if needed. A completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that this text uses a compare and contrast text structure. Remind students that when authors compare, they show how two things are similar. When they contrast, they show how two things are different. Direct students to screen 8 (Printable Text p. 6) and have them read the first sentence. Say: Which word shows that the author is contrasting two things? (unlike) Identify other clue words for comparisons and contrasts, such as like, similarly, and different. Ask students to look for these words as they read.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains how things are similar and different”

Text Features Have students preview the images and captions on screen 4 (Printable Text p. 3). Say: In this text, the writer describes ways people talk about culture in the United States. Some people say that the country is like a melting pot. How do these photos help you understand this idea?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photos help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers understand ideas”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read How Do We Talk About Culture?, have them review their completed four-squares. Ask: Are there any squares you had a hard time filling in? Why? Invite students to share and add to their responses.

Display these images from How Do We Talk About Culture? to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as immigrants, ancestors, and compare.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between How Do We Talk About Culture? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In How Do We Talk About Culture? the author describes the role of immigrants in American life. What else have you read about immigrants? How did they learn about life in a new culture?
  • The author mentions the idea that the United States is like a melting pot. Have you heard this idea before? What have you read about it?
  • In What Is Culture? you read that there are many different cultures in the United States. How does this text add to the idea?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have students fill in the How Do We Talk About Culture? row with something they learned about ways of thinking about culture in the United States.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Do you agree with the idea that the United States is like a melting pot? Why or why not?
  • Why do some people think that the culture of the United States is like a patchwork quilt? Is this a good comparison? Explain.
  • What other metaphor or comparison would be a good way to explain culture in the United States?

Write About the Big Question

Have students choose the metaphor that they think best describes culture in the United States and write a paragraph to explain their answer. Explain that students can choose an example from the text or create their own metaphor.

Cross-Cultural Kids

by Kevin Ruiz
Informational Text - Word Count: 619   Lexile: 790L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the impact that multiple cultures can have on a person’s life.

  • Language Objective Students will explain what it means to be a cross-cultural kid and evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of that experience.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure explains a concept, using features and examples.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section headings can help them identify the topic or main idea of a section in a text.

Summary

This informational text provides insights into the lives of children who grow up experiencing two or more cultures.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What is culture? Ask: What do you think the word cross-cultural means? Can someone have more than one culture?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words cross-cultural, immigrant, and identity. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: If you go to another country on vacation, are you an immigrant? How many cultures are in a cross-cultural kid’s life? What are some parts of your identity? You may wish to explain the difference between “multicultural” (a word used to describe a society with multiple different cultures) and “cross-cultural” (which usually describes a person or society with two or more main cultures). Often, cross-cultural refers to a deeper connection or comparison between cultures.

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Cross-Cultural Kids might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: How might cross-cultural kids contribute to their communities? How do they adapt to different cultures?

VOCABULARY FOUR-SQUARE

Review with students how to create a vocabulary four-square with a vocabulary word in the center. Explain the four elements around the word: a definition, an example of the word in a sentence, a synonym or antonym, and an example. Tell students to complete a four-square diagram for cross-cultural on a sheet of paper, based on the text. Invite them to use reference materials if needed. A completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This text uses a descriptive text structure. The writer defines and describes cross-cultural kids, using details and examples. Have students read the first paragraph of the “Who Are Cross-Cultural Kids?” section on screen 3 (Printable Text p. 2). Guide them to identify details from the paragraph and explain how each detail helps them understand the concept of cross-cultural kids.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains an idea using details and examples”

Text Features Say: This text uses headings to introduce each section. Look at the first heading. What would you expect to read in this section? Have students preview other headings in the text. Guide students to recognize that each heading introduces the topic of the section and hints at the main idea.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “introduce topic of the section”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Cross-Cultural Kids, have them review their completed four-squares and share them with a partner. Invite students to add details to their four-squares, based on their partners’ responses.

Display these images from Cross-Cultural Kids to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as cross-cultural, immigrant, and identity.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Cross-Cultural Kids and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What informational texts or stories have you read about cross-cultural kids? How did the texts help you understand their experiences?
  • What else have you read about immigrants or immigration in the United States? How did Cross-Cultural Kids add to your understanding of the topic?
  • Think back to What Is Culture? In that text, you learned about aspects of culture that are hard to see. What are some of these aspects? How might they affect cross-cultural kids?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Cross-Cultural Kids row with something they learned about living with more than one culture.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why are cross-cultural kids sometimes called “third-culture kids”? Do you think this is a good word for them? What cultures are a part of their lives?
  • What are some challenges of living with two or more cultures?
  • How do cross-cultural kids affect the culture where they live?

Write About the Big Question

Have students write one to two paragraphs responding to the following questions:

  • What is a cross-cultural kid? Give an example.
  • Are you a cross-cultural kid? Why or why not?

Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Have students review all their Vocabulary Four-Square sheets. Put students into small groups and have them “quiz” each other on their vocabulary words using elements of their four-squares. For example, they could say a synonym and ask their group to name the word, or use the example sentences without the vocabulary word and have the group fill in the blank.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: What is culture? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their four-squares as they can.

  • What did you already know about culture before reading these texts?
  • Has your idea of culture changed? Why or why not?
  • How do cultures influence each other?
  • Which texts do you think were most helpful in answering the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: Interview: Culture in My Life

Have students interview an adult in their life, such as a parent, grandparent, or neighbor. Students should ask about the adult’s culture and how elements of the culture play a role in his or her life. Remind students to review what they wrote in the Write About the Big Question for other texts to help them think of questions to ask. They may also use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their interviews. Students should share audio and a written report about the interview. Encourage them to use vocabulary they learned from the texts in their writing. You may wish to compile all of the interviews in a class book.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
