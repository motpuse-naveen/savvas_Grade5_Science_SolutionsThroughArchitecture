In This Text Set

This text set includes five informational texts. The texts provide information about problems that have been solved by architects in the past and the present, as well as problems architects are solving for the future.

The Importance of Architecture is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign The Importance of Architecture for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the table below to see where specific text features and text structures appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Science Standard

    Generate and compare multiple possible solutions to a problem based on how well each is likely to meet the criteria and constraints of the problem.

  • Informational Text Focus

    In this set, students will read informational texts to recognize how description, compare and contrast, and problem and solution text structure—as well as text features such as captions, charts, photographs, and section headings—help their understanding as they read.

  • Content Objective

    Students will understand the importance of architecture and design in solving problems throughout history and across the world.

  • Language Objective

    Students will describe contributions of architects and architecture throughout history. They will discuss the impact of architecture in solving problems or overcoming challenges.

Big Question

How do architects overcome challenges?

The Importance of Architecture

by Joelle Wagner
Informational Text - Word Count: 653   Lexile: 870L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple possible solutions to a problem based on how well each is likely to meet the criteria and constraints of the problem.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the type of work an architect does and the architect’s role in planning and building a structure.

  • Language Objective Students will explain the role of an architect in planning and construction of structures.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure can provide details to show the type of work done by an architect.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs help readers understand the parts of a structure.

Summary

This text explains the importance of architects and the work they do. It addresses aesthetics, as well as function.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? Say: An architect is someone who designs buildings, or plans how they will look. How do you think The Importance of Architecture will help you think about the Big Question? What are some problems or challenges that architects face, and how do you think they solve them?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that include the words architecture, durable, and structure. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: Architecture is the art of planning or designing buildings. What are some examples of architecture? If something is durable, it is strong and will last. What are some durable things you can think of? A structure is something that is built, such as a house or a bridge. What is another example of a structure?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in The Importance of Architecture includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Why is the design of a building important? What kind of building would you want to design?

Cluster Diagram

Display a cluster diagram graphic organizer made up of a central circle with lines and additional circles extending from the central circle. Write the word architecture in the largest circle. As the class reads the text, have students add a word related to architecture in another circle branching off from the largest one, and add additional circles with related words or examples. Model how to branch out different parts of the diagram to show connections between words. A sample diagram might look like this:

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that The Importance of Architecture is written with a descriptive structure. Say: A text written with a descriptive structure provides specific details to help the reader understand and visualize information.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “gives details and examples about what something is or what it is like”

Text Features Say: Photographs are valuable features in a text. In a text about architecture, they can help readers understand the parts of a structure. Direct students’ attention to the photograph of the pagoda. Point out the caption or clickable hotspots, and ask a student to read the text aloud. Ask: What does the photo help you understand about pagodas? Why do you think the author chose to include the picture instead of only describing this structure?

Return to the T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the right column, write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about photographs and how they help readers understand what they read. For example: “help readers understand the parts of a structure”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read The Importance of Architecture, have them review their completed cluster diagrams. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from The Importance of Architecture to help students as they think about words such as architecture, structure, and design.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among The Importance of Architecture and other texts they have read about related subjects.

  • In The Importance of Architecture, you learned about goals many architects have. What have you read in other texts about goals of builders and others who take pride in their work?
  • The Importance of Architecture describes architects solving problems. What are some other texts you have read that tell about solving problems?
  • The Importance of Architecture explains how an architect can help society. What are some other texts you have read that provide details about ways to help society?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solutions through Architecture: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in The Importance of Architecture row with something they learned about solutions created by architects.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • How might an architect overcome the challenge of building a home in an area with a lot of trees or wildlife? How could the architect avoid harming nature?
  • Suppose you were an architect. What is a problem you would like to solve? What would be some ideas for solving the problem?

Write About The Big Question

Tell students: Imagine that you are an architect. You are preparing a presentation to give at a school to explain how you overcome challenges. In your presentation, write a description of what you do and why your work is important. Ask students to include a diagram or diagrams to show a structure they have built, with labels.

Learning from Ancient Architects

by Tony Cavallero
Informational Text - Word Count: 572   Lexile: 990L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple possible solutions to a problem based on how well each is likely to meet the criteria and constraints of the problem.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize the way modern architects have been influenced by the builders of the past.

  • Language Objective Students will explain features of architecture and materials used in construction throughout history, and they will discuss the influence of ancient architecture on structures in the present day.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure helps show how things are similar and different.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize how photographs can help to clarify and provide additional information and details in a text.

Summary

This text explains the way architects throughout history have solved problems through choices in design and building materials.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? Ask: How do you think Learning from Ancient Architects will help you think about the Big Question? How might styles or materials from ancient architecture help architects today?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the following words: materials, design, and structure. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: What materials might you use for an art project? What kind of building would you like to design or plan? What kinds of structures do you think an ancient architect might build?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Learning from Ancient Architects includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. Say: Durable material is strong. Why would an architect want to use durable materials?

Cluster Diagram

Demonstrate creating a cluster diagram and have students create diagrams of their own. In a circle, write architecture, and add a connected circle with the words influence of ancient styles. As the class reads the text, have students add details. Model how to branch out different parts of the diagram to show connections between related words and ideas. A sample diagram might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication Invention and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] smartphone [insert the following text in left column:] make phone calls and use the Internet

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Learning from Ancient Architects is written with a compare and contrast structure. Say: This writer describes similarities and differences between ancient and modern buildings. As you read, look for clue words that show you are reading about a comparison or contrast. Read the first two paragraphs of the Roman Architecture section. Ask: Which words show that the author is comparing two things, or showing how they are similar? (Sample responses: another, similar)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about a compare and contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps show similarities between different styles or times in history,” “helps show how today is different from the past”

Text Features Say: Photographs are important features in many texts. They can give new information or help the reader understand information in the text. Direct students' attention to the photos of the Parthenon and the Lincoln Memorial. Say: The design of the Lincoln Memorial was influenced by Greek building styles. Look at both photographs. What similarities do you see?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about how photographs help them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “help show similarities between different styles or times in history,” “help show how today is different from the past”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Learning from Ancient Architects, have them review their completed vocabulary word webs. Ask: Are there any more words or phrases you would like to add?

Display these images from Learning from Ancient Architects to help students as they think about words such as architect, structure, and materials.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Learning from Ancient Architects and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Learning from Ancient Architects, you learned about challenges in construction that can be solved through architecture, such as using concrete to make longer-lasting buildings. What other texts have you read about architects’ challenges or solutions?
  • In Learning from Ancient Architects, you read about famous structures that currently exist in the United States. What have you read in other texts about these buildings? What have you read about other famous buildings or structures?
  • In The Importance of Architecture, you read about goals that architects have. How did Learning from Ancient Architects help you understand architects’ goals?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solutions through Architecture: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Learning from Ancient Architects row with something they learned about ways architects solve problems.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What challenges do you think Greek or Roman architects faced? How did they resolve them?
  • Which of the structures you read about in this text do you think did the best job of solving problems? Why?

Write About The Big Question

Have students imagine that they are Greek or Roman architects. Ask them to write a letter to a family member to describe their work and the challenges they face. Ask them to describe how they plan to solve or overcome the challenges.

Green Architecture

adapted from Elevate Science
Informational Text - Word Count: 434   Lexile: 900L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple possible solutions to a problem based on how well each is likely to meet the criteria and constraints of the problem.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the work of architects in providing solutions to environmental issues.

  • Language Objective Students will explain architectural features and materials that contribute to structures that are good for the environment.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem and solution structure identifies problems and provides solutions to those problems.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize how charts can help to make text details clearer and more meaningful.

Summary

This text examines environmental problems that can be created by many types of structures. It details specific methods for solving those problems and helping the environment.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? Ask: How do you think Green Architecture will help you think about the Big Question? What are some environmental challenges that architects might face? How could they solve these problems?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the following words: modern and efficient. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: When something is modern, it’s associated with life or styles today. What kinds of buildings look modern to you? When a home is efficient, it uses less energy. How might someone build an efficient home?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Green Architecture includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: How can architects create buildings that are both beautiful and functional?

Cluster Diagram

Demonstrate creating a cluster diagram as shown below and have students copy the structure on a piece of paper. In the largest circle, write architecture. Add another circle with a description or characteristic of green architecture, such as energy-efficient features. Add other circles with related words or examples. Point out that students will add to their diagrams during reading as the class discusses vocabulary words. A sample diagram might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Tell students that Green Architecture is written with a problem and solution structure. Say: This structure identifies a problem and explains one or more ways to solve the problem. Green architecture is architecture that does not harm the environment. What is one problem it might solve?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem and Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about problem and solution structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “describes an important problem or issue,” “tells how a problem could be solved”

Text Features Say: Charts can be extremely useful features in a text. They can clarify information, and they can also add information that will emphasize important points in a text. Direct students’ attention to the pie charts on screen 2 (Printable Text p. x). Have a volunteer read aloud the title of each chart. Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write "Charts." Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about how charts help them understand a writer’s ideas. For example: “help readers visualize or picture information”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Green Architecture, have them review their completed vocabulary cluster diagrams. Ask: Are there any more words or phrases you would like to add?

Display these images from Green Architecture to help students as they think about words such as efficient, modern, and architect.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Green Architecture and other texts they have read about related subjects.

  • In Green Architecture, you learned about considering solutions to help the environment when designing and building a structure. What other texts have you read about the effects of architecture on the environment?
  • In Green Architecture, you read about solar power solving problems. What have you read in other texts about alternative energy sources?
  • Think back to The Importance of Architecture. In that text, you read about what architects have to consider when they plan a building or structure. How did Green Architecture add to your understanding?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solutions through Architecture: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Green Architecture row with something they learned about ways architects solve problems.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are some challenges that green architects might face?
  • Do you predict that the use of solar power and wind power will increase or decrease in the future? How might these types of energy help solve problems?

Write About the Big Question

Tell students to write a persuasive paragraph to a person or company building a house or other building. Their paragraphs should be written to convince the person or company to use an environmentally friendly design. Students should include details about specific design features that could help save energy.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Large Cluster Diagram Have students review their vocabulary cluster diagrams. As a class, you may wish to combine them into one large cluster diagram to show how all of the words and concepts are related. Ask students to write a paragraph about what they learned about architectural features or styles, using words from the cluster diagram.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion related to the Big Question: How do architects overcome challenges? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the pieces they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their word webs as they can.

  • What did you already know about architects overcoming challenges before you began reading this text set?
  • Throughout the years, you’ve likely read other texts about architecture, and perhaps you’ve read about the way designs for cities are planned. Discuss some of the new information you’ve learned.
  • What would you still like to learn about architecture and cities of the future?
  • Which text was most useful in helping you answer the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: You Are the Architect!

Tell students: The future has arrived! You are an architect, and you are planning a design for a city.

  • Write a plan telling your design for the city.
  • Include at least three diagrams to show the ways you will solve problems that cities often experience. Include labels for your diagrams.
  • Create a sketch or a computer-generated image to show your city of the future.
  • Write at least four paragraphs. In your paragraphs, do the following:
    • Discuss reasons and evidence to show why your design plan will be helpful to the environment.
    • Clearly explain details of your design plan.
    • Persuade others of the importance of following your design plan.

Students can refer to information they have written in Write About the Big Question for each text to aid their thinking and planning. Students may use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their projects. Encourage students to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
